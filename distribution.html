üìö Understanding Distribution Shape

üîî What is Normal Distribution?

A normal distribution is symmetric and bell-shaped. It's the most important distribution in statistics because many natural phenomena follow this pattern.

Characteristics: Mean = Median = Mode

Why it matters: Many statistical tests assume normality. If data isn't normal, we need different methods.

‚ÜòÔ∏è Right Skew (Positive Skew)

Data clusters on the left with a long tail extending to the right.

Mean > Median | Skewness > 0

Geography Examples:

  • Income (most earn average, few earn very high)
  • House prices (most affordable, few mansions)
  • Rainfall (mostly dry days, some very wet)

‚ÜôÔ∏è Left Skew (Negative Skew)

Data clusters on the right with a long tail extending to the left.

Mean < Median | Skewness < 0

Geography Examples:

  • Age at death in developed countries (most live long)
  • Test scores (if test is easy, most score high)
  • Retirement age (most retire at normal age, few very early)

üìà Q-Q Plot Interpretation

A Q-Q (Quantile-Quantile) plot compares your data against a theoretical normal distribution:

  • Points on diagonal: Data is normal
  • Curved upward: Right-skewed data
  • Curved downward: Left-skewed data
  • S-shaped: Heavy tails (more extremes than normal)

üéØ What to Do With Non-Normal Data

  • Transform: Log transformation often fixes right skew
  • Use non-parametric tests: Mann-Whitney, Kruskal-Wallis
  • Bootstrap: Resampling methods don't assume normality
  • Report carefully: Note the skewness in your analysis
